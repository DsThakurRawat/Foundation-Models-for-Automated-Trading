People conflate two very different things under the banner "foundation models for trading." There are Time Series Foundation Models — pretrained on billions of raw numerical sequences across domains. And there are LLMs applied to finance — text-first models that reason over news, filings, and earnings calls. But there is a third, fast-growing category that this edition now treats as first-class: event-stream microstructure models — transformers trained on the raw order-flow of markets, the way HRT and TradeFM treat the limit order book "as a language." Most serious work in 2026 lives at the intersection, supported by a deep stack of classical machine-learning tools that this reference also catalogs.

Note — TSFM vs event-stream The models above forecast over evenly-sampled numeric series (OHLCV bars). They are not the same as the order-flow / message-stream models in §08 (DeepLOB, LOBS5, TradeFM), which model the irregular, discrete event stream of the limit order book itself. Don't mix the two paradigms: a TSFM consumes a bar; an event-stream model consumes an order placement, cancellation, or fill.

§ 08Microstructure & order-book deep learning

A more specialized but very important sub-field — and, as of 2026, the frontier. Most HFT and execution alpha lives here, and it is where HRT and JPMorgan are pushing the "foundation model" idea hardest. → See the full Roadmap

Classical supervised LOB models

Paper / ToolNotes
DeepLOB (Zhang et al., 2018)CNN-LSTM for limit-order-book prediction. ~60K params. The starting point everyone benchmarks against.
arXiv:1808.03668
Multi-Horizon LOB Forecasting (2020)Encoder-decoder for LOB with multiple time horizons.
arXiv:2105.10430
TransLOB / LOBTransformerEarly transformer-based LOB modeling.
arXiv:2003.00130
TLOB (Berti & Kasneci, 2025)Dual-attention transformer for price-trend prediction; also documents that LOB predictability is declining over time as markets grow more efficient. Read this before chasing alpha.

Event-stream / generative microstructure foundation models — the HRT paradigm

These treat the raw stream of order-book events — placements, cancellations, fills — as a sequence of "tokens" and train a generative transformer on it, exactly as you'd train an LLM on text. This is the paradigm Marc Khoury (HRT) described at ICML 2025 and that TradeFM (JPMorgan) made concrete in the open literature.

WorkWhat it is & link
HRT — "Foundation Models for Automated Trading" (Khoury, ICML 2025)The talk that named the paradigm. Transformers on full LOB + trade + order events, 20+ years across equities/futures/crypto, ~100 TB ≈ "trillions of tokens"; reports LLM-style scaling laws. Not open, but the clearest statement of the thesis.
SlidesLive talk · AI Street writeup
TradeFM (Kawawa-Beaudan et al., JPMorgan, ICLR 2026)The open analog: a 524M-param decoder-only generative transformer on billions of trade events across 9,000+ equities, with a scale-invariant universal tokenizer; rollouts reproduce stylized facts and generalize zero-shot to APAC markets. Under review — blueprint, not codebase. → Roadmap
OpenReview:ODdcZz7VnY · arXiv:2602.23784
LOBS5 (Nagy et al., 2023)The reproducible cornerstone. First end-to-end autoregressive generative model of tokenized LOB messages, using S5 (structured state-space) layers; ~6.3M params; digit-level tokenizer on LOBSTER data; decoded by JAX-LOB.
peernagy/LOBS5 · arXiv:2309.00638
JAX-LOB (Frey et al., 2023)GPU-accelerated LOB matching-engine simulator — processes thousands of books in parallel, ~75x faster per message. The deterministic engine that decodes generated messages and powers RL.
KangOxford/jax-lob · arXiv:2308.13289
LOB-Bench (2025)Benchmark for generative LOB models — distributional fidelity via Wasserstein distance on interarrival times, order depths, book imbalance, spreads, returns. The honest eval target. → Roadmap: Phase 5
arXiv:2502.09172
TradeFM-class event tokenizersTwo competing tokenization philosophies to ablate: digit-level (LOBS5) vs feature-binning (TradeFM, e.g. [liquidity, participant, Δprice-bin, trade-bin]). This choice dominates results. → Roadmap: Phase 3
LOBSTERStandard academic limit-order-book message dataset (NASDAQ). The default training data for LOBS5.
lobsterdata.com

ML / serving

§ 15Major achievements — a timeline

Red nodes are the broad field; teal nodes trace the event-stream microstructure lineage that the Roadmap builds on.

  • 2017Transformer paper (Vaswani et al.). Everything downstream depends on this.
  • 2018DeepLOB — CNN-LSTM for limit-order books, the first major DL breakthrough in microstructure.
  • 2019FinBERT: domain-tuned BERT for financial sentiment, the de facto baseline for years.
  • 2020FinRL launched — first major open-source RL framework for finance.
  • 2021–2022Informer, Autoformer, PatchTST — the time-series transformer wave that made TSFMs possible.
  • March 2023BloombergGPT released — first frontier-scale finance LLM.
  • April 2023Lopez-Lira & Tang — the "Can ChatGPT predict stock returns?" paper that sparked the trading-LLM wave.
  • June 2023FinGPT released — open-source via LoRA, dropping fine-tuning cost from ~$5M to under $300.
  • Aug–Sep 2023JAX-LOB + LOBS5 (Oxford) — a GPU LOB simulator and the first end-to-end generative model of tokenized order-book messages. The open event-stream stack is born.
  • Late 2023FinMem introduces layered memory for LLM trading agents.
  • Feb 2024FinAgent — first major multimodal foundation agent for trading.
  • March 2024Chronos by Amazon — time-series tokenization breakthrough.
  • May 2024TimesFM by Google — patched-decoder TSFM trained on Google production data.

§ 16A suggested learning path

For the general field, the path below. For the specific build — event-stream microstructure models the HRT/TradeFM way — follow the companion Roadmap instead, which turns this into a phased implementation plan.

  1. Foundations. Read Advances in Financial Machine Learning (López de Prado), then Machine Learning for Algorithmic Trading (Jansen) for working code. Add Inside the Black Box (Narang) for cultural context.
  2. Survey the field. Skim the FinLLMs survey, the LLM-Agent-in-Trading survey, and the TSFM survey. These will save you months of arbitrary paper-hopping.
  3. Get hands dirty. Clone FinRL, run the examples. Then TradingAgents to feel the agent paradigm.
  4. Forecasting. Try Chronos and TimesFM via gluonts on real stock data. Compare against ARIMA, Prophet, and DLinear baselines — you will be humbled.
  5. Microstructure. Read DeepLOB and TLOB, then clone LOBS5 and JAX-LOB. This is the on-ramp to the Roadmap.
  6. Backtesting discipline. Learn vectorbt or NautilusTrader; read López de Prado on cross-validation for finance and the deflated Sharpe ratio.
  7. Validate ruthlessly. Walk-forward analysis, transaction-cost modeling, regime testing, combinatorial purged CV. Skip this and you are curve-fitting.

An honest caveat The published papers and open-source repos showing "X% returns" almost always have problems — lookahead bias, survivorship bias, no transaction costs, benchmark contamination. The frameworks are excellent for learning and as research scaffolds. None are turnkey money machines. The firms that make money keep their actual models private. This is a technical landscape, not investment advice, and "the model said so" remains the worst reason to trade.
