There are three things people call "foundation models for trading," and only one is the subject here. Not TSFMs on price bars (Ref §04), not LLMs on filings (Ref §05) — but transformers trained on the raw event stream: every order placement, cancellation, and fill, tokenized and modelled the way an LLM models text. This is what HRT presented at ICML 2025 and what TradeFM made concrete in the open. What follows is the whole build, not a summary of it.

Part I · Orientation

§ 1The thesis & what you're building

Strip the branding and the bet is simple: model the market as a sequence of events and pretrain a transformer on it like a language model. Three claims follow, each with outside corroboration:

  • Tokenize the order flow. Each add, cancel, or fill becomes a token. HRT names two bundling schemes — fixed time-windows or fixed event-counts — each with cost/performance tradeoffs.
  • Scaling laws hold. More data and bigger models keep improving the model, the same curve seen in LLMs. TradeFM independently confirms data-scaling holds for microstructure.
  • The structure is universal. TradeFM generalizes zero-shot from US equities to APAC markets — the "physics" of order books is consistent enough that one pretrained model transfers.

The honest limitation, stated by TradeFM itself: the model is partially observant — it never sees hidden intent — and turning it into a trader needs RL fine-tuning so it optimizes rather than merely mimics. Hold that in mind through every phase: a perfect mimic of the past is not a profitable agent in the future.

§ 2Prerequisites, skills & glossary

Be honest about which of these you have before starting; the gaps tell you where Phase 0 reading goes.

  • Deep learning. Transformers from scratch, autoregressive training, mixed precision, distributed training basics. JAX or PyTorch fluency (the open stack is JAX-heavy).
  • Market microstructure. What a limit order book is, order types, price-time priority, the difference between a quote and a trade. Larry Harris's Trading and Exchanges covers this (Ref §14).
  • Data / streaming systems. Websockets, message ordering, idempotent ingestion, columnar storage. This is the part where your background is the edge, not a gap.
  • Statistics for finance. Stylized facts, heavy tails, stationarity, why naive cross-validation leaks in time series.
  • Reinforcement learning (only if targeting trading): policy gradients, reward shaping, sim-to-real.

§ 3Scoping, three goals & budget reality

HRT did ~$12.3B in 2025 net trading revenue and runs a datacenter so large its GPU orders reportedly affected regional supply. You are not reproducing that. Their scale ladder, for calibration: DeepLOB ~60K params → LOBS5 ~6.3M → TradeFM ~500M → HRT "trillions of tokens." A solo build lives at the LOBS5-to-small-TradeFM end — that is enough to prove every idea.

Pick which of these you're building before Phase 0, because it changes the later phases:

GoalOptimizes forEnds at
Learning / portfolioUnderstanding + a credible public artifactPhase 5 — a model that passes LOB-Bench, written up.
Open-source simulatorA reusable high-fidelity market simulatorPhases 2–5, polished and released. The most defensible solo outcome.
Real trading attemptA deployable policyPhases 6–7 — and aim at execution, not directional alpha.

Budget & hardware, concretely

  • Phases 0–1: free. Colab / one consumer GPU is enough for FI-2010 baselines and LOBS5-scale experiments.
  • Phase 2 (data): near-free for crypto; storage is the cost — budget for terabytes if you collect full depth on several venues for months.
  • Phase 4 (pretrain): the real spend. A ~10–50M-param model on billions of tokens is single- to few-GPU and rentable for low hundreds of dollars per run; a ~500M TradeFM-scale model is multi-GPU and runs into the thousands. Rent A100/H100 by the hour, use spot/preemptible instances, and never buy hardware for this.
Reframe — the durable deliverable A directional predictor decays; TLOB measured LOB predictability falling year over year. A market simulator does not: TradeFM's own pitch is synthetic data, stress testing, and training RL agents. For a solo builder the durable artifact is the simulator, not a money printer. Optimize for that and the trading option stays open without being the thing you bet the project on.

The field splits into a classical supervised lineage and the new generative event-stream lineage. The Reference catalogues both in §08; here is what each contributes.

Part II · The build, layer by layer

§ 6Data — sourcing, reconstruction, quality, leakage

This is 30–40% of the project and where most builds silently fail. A normalization leak or an undetected gap corrupts everything downstream and produces results that look great and mean nothing.

Sources compared

SourceTypeNotes
Crypto exchange websockets (via cryptofeed)Free · L2/L3Full depth, 24/7, no licensing barrier. Per-exchange quirks; you reconstruct the book yourself.
LOBSTERPaid · MBONASDAQ message data; the exact format LOBS5 expects. Academic licensing.
databentoPaid · MBO/MBPAffordable full-depth US equity/futures; cleanest path to real equity LOB.
Tardis.dev / CoinAPIPaid · cryptoPre-collected historical crypto books if you don't want to run a collector for months.
Polygon / AlpacaFreemiumGood for bars and trades; limited true depth.

Choose your granularity first

MBO (every individual order, with IDs) preserves queue position and is what the generative models want; MBP-10 (aggregated top-10 levels) is smaller and enough for many supervised tasks; L1 is too coarse for this work. Decide up front — it dictates storage volume and tokenizer design.

Order-book reconstruction (the part people underestimate)

  • Most feeds give a snapshot + incremental deltas. You apply deltas in sequence-number order to maintain a live book.
  • Track sequence numbers and detect gaps; on a gap, discard and re-snapshot — never silently interpolate.
  • Validate with the exchange checksum where provided (OKX, Kraken, others publish a periodic book checksum); a mismatch means your book has drifted and that segment is poison.
  • Handle reconnection, rate limits, and the snapshot/delta race (buffer deltas until the snapshot arrives, then replay).

Data quality & cleaning

  • Timestamps: prefer exchange time over local receive time; record both; expect out-of-order arrivals.
  • Gaps, halts, auctions: mark them explicitly — never let a halt look like a quiet market.
  • Equities only: adjust for splits/dividends and handle the opening/closing auction as a distinct regime.
  • Crypto only: filter exchange outages, obvious wash/spoof bursts, and depeg/flash-crash artifacts unless you specifically want them.

Storage architecture & volume math

Three tiers: raw capture (append-only, immutable, exactly as received) → normalized event store (one schema across venues, Parquet/Arrow in Polars or QuestDB/ClickHouse) → training shards (tokenized, fixed-length, ready for the loader). A single active crypto pair can emit millions of events per day; full depth across several venues is multi-GB/day, terabytes over months. Compress aggressively and keep the raw tier append-only so you can always re-derive.

Leakage is the silent killer Split strictly by time, never randomly. Fit every normalization statistic (means, bins, scalers) on the training window only, then apply forward — fitting a scaler on the full set leaks the future into the past. Insert an embargo / purge gap between train and test so a long event sequence can't straddle the boundary. If your backtest looks too good, suspect leakage before you suspect genius.

Versioning & legal

Hash and version datasets (DVC or content hashes) so a result is reproducible months later. Check redistribution terms: LOBSTER is academic-licensed, exchange data has ToS, and you generally cannot republish raw vendor data — you can usually publish derived statistics and trained weights.

§ 7Tokenization — the crux

This is the single highest-leverage decision in the whole project. You are turning an irregular, multi-modal, heterogeneous event stream into a discrete sequence a transformer can model. Get it wrong and no amount of model scale recovers.

The three schemes

  • Digit-level (LOBS5). Tokenize the actual numbers — price and size — digit-group by digit-group, like LLM number tokenization. Simple, reversible, but long sequences and weak cross-asset transfer.
  • Feature-binning (TradeFM). Map each event to a small vector of discretized indicators, e.g. [liquidity, participant, Δprice-bin, trade-bin]. This is what buys scale-invariance and zero-shot transfer across assets. More design work; the winning approach for a universal model.
  • Structured-message. Treat each message as a typed record (event-type + fields) with sub-tokens. A middle ground; closest to how the book actually updates.

Time representation

Markets are not evenly sampled, so you must encode time. Three options, all worth ablating: fixed time-windows (one token per Δt — HRT scheme A), fixed event-counts (one token per N events — HRT scheme B), or an explicit Δt token / time embedding alongside each event. The choice changes what "context length" even means.

Scale-invariance — the hard requirement

BTC at $60,000 and a $5 stock must share one vocabulary or the model can't transfer. The fixes: encode relative price changes (basis points, not dollars), tick-normalize, and log-bin sizes. TradeFM's universal tokenizer is essentially this discipline made systematic.

Vocabulary, special tokens, sequence length, reversibility

  • Size the vocabulary deliberately — too small loses information, too large starves rare tokens.
  • Add special tokens: sequence start/end, level boundaries, day/session boundaries.
  • Pick a context length that captures real dependencies without blowing up compute — this is exactly why LOBS5 uses S5 for long sequences.
  • Reversibility is mandatory for simulation: a generated token sequence must decode into a valid book update the matching engine can apply. Test this before training anything large.
Ablation protocolBuild at least the digit-level and feature-binning tokenizers, hold the model fixed, and compare vocabulary size, sequence length, perplexity, and downstream LOB-Bench fidelity. This comparison is itself a publishable mini-result.

§ 8Model & training

Architecture

Decoder-only transformer (TradeFM, HRT) is the default for generation. S5 / structured state-space (LOBS5) handles very long event sequences far more cheaply — strongly consider it given how long tokenized order flow gets; Mamba is a newer SSM alternative. Use time-aware positional encoding (rotary or an explicit time embedding) since position ≠ time here. Consider multi-task output heads — next-event plus an auxiliary price-direction or size head — which can stabilize and sharpen representations.

Training

  • Objective: autoregressive next-token. Mix data across assets and regimes so the model learns universal structure, not one symbol's quirks.
  • Sizing & the scale ladder: start at ~10–50M params (already past LOBS5, far below TradeFM). Prove the loop, then run a 3-point size ladder and verify your loss bends like a scaling law before spending on a big run.
  • Distributed: data-parallel first; FSDP / ZeRO or JAX sharding when the model outgrows one GPU.
  • Hygiene: LR warmup + cosine decay, gradient clipping, mixed precision with NaN guards, frequent checkpointing with clean resume, and full experiment tracking (W&B / MLflow). Log perplexity and divergence continuously.

§ 9Simulation & the matching engine

The simulator decodes generated messages into book states and is also where RL lives. Reuse JAX-LOB rather than writing your own unless engineering the engine is your goal.

  • Closed loop: model proposes a message → engine applies it → updated state feeds back as context. This loop is where error accumulation appears (see §14).
  • Mechanics that matter: price-time priority, partial fills, queue position, and — if you ever go live — latency and your own market impact.
  • Unit-test the engine. It is the one component where a subtle bug silently fabricates profit. Test matching, cancels, and partial fills against hand-worked examples.
Simulators lie in known waysJAX-LOB makes simplifying choices (e.g. interpreting some large messages as an execution plus a new limit order). Know your engine's assumptions before trusting any result that depends on them — a "profitable" policy can be an artifact of the simulator, not the market.

§ 10Evaluation — the five layers

A model can pass one layer and fail the next. Climb them in order; do not skip ahead to PnL.

  1. Intrinsic. Perplexity and next-token accuracy. Necessary, not sufficient — a low-perplexity model can still generate unrealistic markets.
  2. Distributional fidelity / stylized facts. Roll out generations and check heavy tails, volatility clustering, absence of return autocorrelation, and intraday seasonality. Score on LOB-Bench (Wasserstein distance on interarrival times, depths, imbalance, spreads, returns). This is the real bar for a simulator.
  3. Simulation realism. Rollout stability over long horizons and correct market-impact response curves. A model whose simulated market diverges into nonsense fails here even with good perplexity.
  4. Predictive skill. Only now compare against the Phase-1 DeepLOB/TLOB baselines, on held-out and out-of-distribution data (a different time period, a different venue, an unseen asset).
  5. Economic value (trading only). Cost-adjusted PnL, Sharpe, drawdown — and trust the deflated versions (§12), never the raw backtest.

§ 11From model to trading

Optional, and only after Layers 1–4 pass. For a solo build, aim at execution, not directional alpha — execution is more tractable, less adversarial, and the place a microstructure model has a real edge.

  • Setup: wrap the model as a policy on JAX-LOB via AlphaTrade; or use FinRL for a higher-level harness (Ref §07).
  • Reward design: implementation shortfall minus an inventory-risk penalty for execution; be very careful that the reward can't be gamed by a simulator quirk.
  • The two ceilings: the sim-to-real gap (a policy tuned on a simulator overfits its assumptions) and partial observability (you never see hidden intent). Both cap how far this goes; respect them.
Part III · Doing it safely

§ 12Validation discipline

The literature is a graveyard of impressive backtests that were artifacts. These practices are what separate a real result from a curve fit.

  • Walk-forward only. Train on the past, test on the strictly-later future, roll forward. Never shuffle time.
  • Combinatorial purged cross-validation with embargo (López de Prado) so overlapping samples can't leak across the train/test boundary.
  • Model costs explicitly: commissions, spread, slippage, and your own market impact. Most "alpha" dies here.
  • Deflated Sharpe ratio and probability of backtest overfitting (PBO) to correct for the many strategies you implicitly tried.
  • Regime & stress testing: evaluate separately across calm, volatile, trending, and crisis periods — an average that hides a blow-up is worse than useless.

§ 15Engineering & MLOps

  • Repo & config: separate data/ tokenize/ model/ sim/ eval/ trade/; every run driven by a versioned config file, no magic constants.
  • Pipeline orchestration: data → tokenize → train → eval as reproducible, resumable stages (Make, Prefect, or simple DAG scripts).
  • Tracking & registry: W&B / MLflow for runs; a model registry so a checkpoint is traceable to its data hash, config, and eval scores.
  • Testing: unit-test the matching engine and the tokenizer's reversibility above all; integration-test the data pipeline against known-good days.
  • Cost monitoring: tag GPU spend per experiment; a dashboard prevents the quiet four-figure surprise.
Part IV · Sequencing

§ 16The phased plan & go/no-go gates

Each phase has an output and a gate — an explicit condition for proceeding, or for killing the project. A real plan tells you when to stop.

  1. Internalize the frame

    ~1–2 weeks · reading, no code

    Read LOBS5 and TradeFM end to end; skim TLOB and LiT; read Harris on order types and matching (Ref §14). Be able to whiteboard why a burst of cancels at the best bid is a token with predictive content.

    Gate: you can explain the LOB-as-language thesis and the MBO/MBP distinction unaided. If not, stay here.

  2. Supervised baseline on a fixed benchmark

    ~3–4 weeks · PyTorch · single GPU/Colab

    Get FI-2010 (Ref §10) and reproduce DeepLOB, then TLOB, for mid-price direction. Gives a known-good harness and a number to beat.

    Gate: your F1 matches the published DeepLOB/TLOB numbers. If you can't reproduce a known result, fix that before generating anything.

  3. Build your own event corpus

    ~2–4 weeks setup, then ongoing · your edge

    Stand up the cryptofeed + ccxt collector, write reconstruction with gap detection and checksum validation, store as a compact event log. Treat it as a streaming-systems problem.

    Gate: a week of data reconstructs to books that pass exchange checksums with zero unexplained gaps. Dirty data here ruins everything later.

  4. Tokenization — the hard part

    ~3–6 weeks · dominates results

    Build scale-invariant digit-level and feature-binning tokenizers; ablate them and the time-bundling schemes; verify reversibility against the engine.

    Gate: tokens decode back into valid books, and one scheme clearly wins on perplexity/fidelity. Don't pretrain on a tokenizer you haven't validated.

  5. Pretrain a small generative model

    ~4–8 weeks · rented A100/H100

    Decoder-only or S5, next-token, ~10–50M params. Prove the loop, then verify your scaling curve on a 3-point ladder before any larger run.

    Gate: loss scales the right way on the ladder. If bigger doesn't help at small scale, a big run won't save it — stop and revisit data/tokenizer.

  6. Evaluate — generation before prediction

    ~3–4 weeks · LOB-Bench + stylized facts

    Run the five-layer evaluation (§10): stylized facts and LOB-Bench first, predictive skill against Phase-1 baselines second.

    Gate: the model reproduces stylized facts and posts a competitive LOB-Bench score. This is the natural finish line for the learning/simulator goals.

  7. Trading — only now

    ~4–8 weeks · AlphaTrade / FinRL

    Wrap as an execution policy on JAX-LOB. Validate with the full §12 discipline: walk-forward, purged CV, costs, deflated Sharpe, regime tests.

    Gate: positive cost-adjusted, deflated performance across multiple regimes. Anything less stays in simulation — do not risk capital.

  8. Package & share

    ~2 weeks · the part most people skip

    Clean the repo, write the README and a short technical post, release the simulator or eval harness. A working open simulator + tokenizer is a genuinely useful artifact regardless of the trading result.

    Gate: someone else can clone it and reproduce one headline number from your writeup.
